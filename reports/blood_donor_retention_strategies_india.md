# Blood Donor Retention Strategies: Indian Context
### A Research-Based Strategy Reference

## Section 1: Foundational Principles

---

### 1.1 Prioritise Retention Over Recruitment

**Basis:** [van Dongen] states: *"Most blood collection establishments spend more money and
effort on recruitment of new donors than on retention of current donors. Unjustifiably so,
because retention has several benefits over recruitment."*

Retention advantages cited:

- **Safety:** *"The risk of transfusion-transmitted viral infections (e.g. HIV, Hepatitis B and
  C) in donor blood is higher in new donors than it is in current donors."* [van Dongen]
- **Cost:** In the Netherlands, *"the estimated costs for recruiting a new donor range from
  22 euro's (ambassador recruitment) to 58 euro's ('cold' recruitment), whilst retaining a
  donor costs a mere 7 euro's per year."* [van Dongen] *(Note: these figures are for the
  Netherlands only; comparable Indian cost data is not available in the reviewed papers.)*
- **Predictability:** *"experienced donors are safer, have significant cost-benefit advantages,
  and allow for accurate forecasting."* [Bagot, citing Devine et al.]

[Grazzini] reinforces this globally: *"Effective donor management and donation systems should
rely on low-risk, regular, voluntary unpaid donors."*

**Recommendation:** Indian blood banks should formally shift resource allocation toward
retention programmes, not only recruitment drives.

---

### 1.2 Understand That Retention and Recruitment Require Different Strategies

**Basis:** [van Dongen]: *"Studies have found that the motivating factors associated with first
time donation are different from the factors associated with maintenance of the blood donation
behaviour."*

[Bagot] confirms: *"Interventions designed to increase blood donation behavior in more
experienced donors may not be as effective when used for the first-time donor."*

**Recommendation:** Indian blood banks must not apply the same strategy uniformly across
first-time, novice, and regular donors.

---

### 1.3 Apply Culturally Adapted Social Marketing

**Basis:** [Grazzini]: *"Blood and plasma donation rates vary significantly by area, location
and community. Different ethnic groups have different donation rates. There are strong
relationships between donation rates and age, gender, education, social class, occupation
and religion."*

And: *"Promoting blood and plasma donation requires identifying the relevant behavioural and
psychological characteristics of donors = what motivates some individuals and not others to
donate."*

[Grazzini] defines social marketing in this context as: *"an approach used to develop
activities aimed at changing or maintaining people's behaviour for the benefit of individuals
and society as a whole."* And: *"Social marketing in blood donation is not about selling or
offering products but about selling a good feeling to donors and to the whole community."*

**Recommendation:** Retention strategies in India must be localised to regional demographic
profiles, cultural norms, and communication preferences. What works in Manipal may differ
from what works in Chandigarh — as evidenced by the divergent SMS vs. phone call
preferences between [Mohammed] and [Malhotra] (see Section 3.1).

## Section 2: Donor Segmentation

---

### 2.1 Focus First on New and First-Time Donors

**Basis:** [van Dongen]: *"lapsing mostly occurs in first time donors, and habit formation only
starts around the fourth donation."*

Lapsing rates from the literature:
- Netherlands: *"24·9% do not provide a second donation."* [van Dongen, citing Sanquin unpublished data]
- England and North Wales: *"only 38·4% made a second donation within 6 months, and
  52·1% made a second donation within 12 months."* [van Dongen, citing Lattimore et al. 2015]
- Ohio: *"only 35% of first and second time donors made a repeat donation attempt within
  2 years."* [van Dongen, citing France et al. 2013]
- Global estimate: *"Only 50% of first-time whole blood (WB) donors return for a subsequent
  donation."* [Bagot]

In India: *"34.8 % [of 13,594 donors] were first-time donors."* [Mohammed] — meaning over
a third of the donor pool is at the highest risk of lapse.

**Recommendation:** Post-first-donation outreach must be treated as the highest-priority
retention window in Indian blood banks.

---

### 2.2 Use Cluster-Based, Targeted Strategies

**Basis:** [Mohammed] directly: *"Currently, donor recruitment efforts largely rely on a general
approach that includes the use of slogans, pamphlets, awareness talks, newspaper articles, and
public announcements. These strategies are typically broad in nature, aiming to appeal to the
general population rather than targeting specific donor segments. As a result, they often lack
personalization and may not effectively address the unique motivations or barriers faced by
different groups within the donor population."*

The [Mohammed] study (n = 13,594, South India) identified **nine donor clusters** through
hierarchical and two-step cluster analysis. The clusters and their recommended strategies
are as follows:

---

#### Cluster 1 — Young male graduates pursuing careers in business (n = 2,044)

*"To make blood donation easy and accessible for working professionals by organizing
workplace donation drives and convenient events near their offices, addressing time as the
main barrier hindering blood donation."*

*"Use personalized texts or phone calls to remind and encourage donors about upcoming drives
and their donation due dates, helping to strengthen connections and increase participation."*

---

#### Cluster 2 — Young male undergraduates donating blood for the first time (n = 1,385)

*"Organizing education and awareness campaigns in schools, colleges, and universities to
clear myth about blood donation, fear and health related doubts."*

*"To Partner with student organizations and social media influencers to create awareness
campaigns that resonate with younger audiences."*

---

#### Cluster 3 — Young employed men donating blood for the first time (n = 1,544)

*"Ensuring a quick and smooth donation process to accommodate and support employed blood
donors effectively."*

---

#### Cluster 4 — Middle-aged men with less than 10 blood donations (n = 2,119)

*"Emphasize the health benefits of regular donations and the positive impact on the
community."*

---

#### Cluster 5 — Young employed men, repeat blood donors (less than 10 times) (n = 1,648)

*"Offering transportation services or mobile donation units to increase accessibility."*

*"Acknowledge milestones and provide updates on how their contributions are making a
difference."*

---

#### Cluster 6 — Male undergraduates, repeat blood donors (n = 1,545)

*"Form blood donor clubs within institutions to maintain a sense of community and shared
purpose."*

*"Identification of Peer influencer; Recruitment of enthusiastic student ambassadors or peer
leaders to advocate for blood donation within their networks."*

---

#### Cluster 7 — Young employed male, repeat blood donors (more than 10 times) (n = 1,203)

*"Celebrating and appreciate donors' efforts by sharing their contributions on social platforms
to inspire and encourage others."*

*"Regular check-ups and monitoring of the health of regular blood donors to ensure their
well-being and maintain a stable donor pool."*

---

#### Cluster 8 — Female blood donors (n = 819)

*"Addressing fear factors among women by providing clear information, sharing positive
stories, and offering reassurance through supportive counseling and educational campaigns."*

*"Holding promotional campaigns featuring inspiring women donors to share their stories and
create positive emotions, encouraging more women to donate blood."*

---

#### Cluster 9 — Young businessmen, first time blood donors (n = 1,287)

*"Highlighting blood donation as a meaningful and impactful Corporate Social Responsibility
(CSR) activity."*

*"Establishing a database to maintain communication with first-time donors, providing updates
on the impact of their donations and inviting them for future donation drives."*

---

### 2.3 Identify High-Risk Demographics for Lapsing

**Basis:** [van Dongen], citing Dutch data: *"lapsed donors were more often female, younger
than 24 years, had a lower social economic status... and lived in more urbanised areas."*

England and North Wales data: *"return rate increased with increasing age group, men were
more likely to return than women, and whites were more likely to return compared with other
ethnic groups."* [van Dongen, citing Lattimore et al. 2015]

[Mohammed] found 80.6% of the Indian study population were young adults aged 18–39, and
[Malhotra] found 59.2% were aged 18–30 — indicating young adults dominate the Indian donor
pool and therefore represent the key retention risk group.

**Recommendation:** Special attention should be paid to young, first-time, and female donors
in retention programmes. [Mohammed]'s Cluster 8 (all female donors) and Clusters 2 and 3
(first-time young donors) represent the highest-risk groups in the Indian dataset.

## Section 3: Communication and Reminders

---

### 3.1 Use the Right Reminder Channel — Phone Call vs. SMS

Two Indian studies produced different findings on preferred contact channel. Both are
reported exactly:

**[Malhotra] (North India — Chandigarh/Rishikesh):**
*"the most preferred way of contact (83%–97% of donors) were reminder services such as
phone call. Donors observed a personal phone call more motivating than short message service
(sms) or E-mail."*

Males: 95% preferred phone calls vs. 4.32% SMS vs. 0.9% email.
Females: 77% phone call, 16% SMS, 7% email.

**[Mohammed] (South India — Manipal):**
*"Most participants (89.5 %) are receptive to donation reminders, with Short Message Services
(SMS) (51.7 %) and phone calls (28 %) being the preferred modalities."*

**Reconciliation:** These findings are not contradictory — they reflect regional and
demographic differences. [Malhotra] itself states strategies must be *"feasible as per the
socioeconomic and educational status of the local population."* Indian blood banks should
survey their own donor populations for channel preference rather than assuming either
finding applies universally.

**Evidence for effectiveness of reminders generally:** [van Dongen] reports: *"Godin et al.
(2012) found a small-to-medium effect size of reminders (d = 0·36 across seven studies) on
blood donation retention."*

**Caution on overuse:** [Malhotra] cites a study finding that *"phone calls to regular donors
decreased their overall donation rate, whereas it induces a substantial habit forming effect
in FT donors."* Reminder strategies should therefore be targeted primarily at first-time and
lapsed donors, not uniformly applied to all repeat donors.

---

### 3.2 Personalise Reminder Content

**Basis:** [Bagot]: *"praise included with a reminder may be perceived as less meaningful and
sincere than when combined with an explanation of how the prior donation was used."*

[Malhotra] recommends: *"subtle gestures as birthday wishes and honoring them on occasions
such as world blood donor day and national blood donation day greatly acknowledge their
efforts."*

**Recommendation:** Reminder messages should reference the donor's specific donation
history and the impact of their blood, not generic mass messaging.

---

### 3.3 Use Social Media Strategically for Younger Segments

**Basis:** [Mohammed]: *"Individual communication (32.3 %) and social media (31.8 %) were
the most preferred channels for communication."* Social media was preferred particularly
among *"Female, graduate, postgraduates, employed"* donors.

The strategy for Cluster 2 (young undergraduates) recommends: *"To Partner with student
organizations and social media influencers to create awareness campaigns that resonate with
younger audiences."*

**Caution from [Malhotra]:** A cited study found *"reminders using WhatsApp messages does
not play a role to enhance donor return rates."* Social media may be better suited for
awareness and recruitment than operational reminders.

---

## Section 4: Addressing Deterrents

---

### 4.1 Address Fear as the Primary Psychological Barrier

**Basis — Indian data:** [Mohammed]: *"Key factors deterring blood donation included lack of
time (28.7 %) and fear (25.8 %), with specific concerns such as fear of needles (15.1 %) and
anemia (11.9 %)."*

**Basis — global literature:** [van Dongen]: *"One of the most commonly named barriers, even
for regular donors, is anxiety or fear of donating blood. Fear can take many forms, such as
fear of needles, general nervousness, fear of reduced health after donating, or fear of
fainting."*

[Bagot]: *"Almost two-thirds of first-time donors reported being deterred by adverse reactions
(eg, faintness and nausea) occurring either during or after a donation. A fear of needles was
reported by almost half."*

**Recommended interventions:**

- Pre-donation counselling and clear procedure explanation — [Mohammed] Cluster 2 strategy.
- In-center psychological support: [Bagot] reports one intervention that *"increased retention
  from approximately 60% to 96.64%"* through *"a personal interview addressing fears and
  anxieties."* This is described as *"very large effects... for reducing vasovagal reactions
  (d = −1.70) and increasing donor returns (d = 1.70)."*
- For female donors specifically: [Mohammed] Cluster 8 strategy — *"Addressing fear factors
  among women by providing clear information, sharing positive stories, and offering
  reassurance through supportive counseling and educational campaigns."*

---

### 4.2 Reduce Vasovagal Reactions Through Physical Interventions

**Basis:** [van Dongen]: *"France et al. (2005) found that of those donors who did not
experience a vasovagal reaction, 64% returned for a next donation within 1 year, whilst
amongst donors who experienced light vasovagal reactions, only 40% returned for a next
donation."*

And: *"Newman et al. (2006) found that experiencing a vasovagal reaction decreased return
rates by 34%."*

And: *"fatigue decreased return rates by 20%."* [van Dongen, citing Newman et al. 2006]

**Recommended interventions:**

- **Water loading:** *"Applied muscle tension and water loading are interventions that can
  prevent vasovagal reactions."* [van Dongen]
- **Applied muscle tension:** Can be combined with implementation intentions: *"Instructing
  donors to repeat the proposition 'If I am in the donation chair, then I will immediately start
  using applied muscle tension techniques'."* [van Dongen]
- **Caffeine:** [Bagot] notes that for first-time donors specifically, *"behavioral interventions
  involving caffeine were more effective in improving the donation experience than water."*
  This was a single study finding and should be interpreted cautiously.
- **Distraction:** [Bagot] found audiovisual distraction had a *"moderate effect (d = 0.43)
  for those with a threat-avoiding coping style."*

**Recommendation:** Pre-donation water loading should become a standard protocol in Indian
blood banks. Phlebotomists should be trained to instruct applied muscle tension techniques.

---

### 4.3 Train Phlebotomists in Social and Communication Skills

**Basis:** [van Dongen]: *"Another study found that phlebotomists' social skills reduce donor
reactions. These interventions may be most effective when targeted on those donors that
experience high subjective distress."*

And: *"Donors receiving social support during blood donation reported fewer pre-faint
reactions and greater likelihood of donating again within the next year. Social support does
not necessarily have to be provided by a person known to the donor, as this experiment used
research assistants providing encouragement, and distraction in the form of small talk."*

**Recommendation:** Phlebotomist training in Indian blood banks should formally include
communication, reassurance, and light distraction techniques — not only technical skill.

---

### 4.4 Address Planning Failures and Time Constraints

**Basis — Indian data:** [Mohammed]: *"Lack of time (28.7 %)"* was the top deterrent.
Inconvenient location (19.4%) and long waiting times (19.9%) were also prominent.

**Basis — global literature:** [van Dongen]: *"Failure of planning donations, the time it takes
to donate blood, and lack of reminders have all been named as barriers for retention."*

And: *"planning failure... acts as a long term determinant of donation behaviour, by not only
negatively affecting the first consecutive donation but also the second and third donations."*

**Recommended interventions:**

- **Implementation intentions:** Donors who completed structured if-then planning had
  *"an 11·5% higher return rate than donors in the control condition."* [van Dongen, citing
  Wevers et al. 2015]
  Example proposition: *"'If I receive the invitation card, then I will schedule a date and time
  in my agenda to donate blood on (opening hours of the blood bank)'."* [van Dongen]
- **Temporarily deferred donors:** If-then planning gave deferred donors *"a 19% greater
  chance of returning to give blood again compared with the control condition."* [van Dongen,
  citing Godin et al. 2013]
- **Workplace donation drives:** [Mohammed] Cluster 1 strategy — specifically for working
  professionals.
- **Mobile donation units:** [Mohammed] Cluster 5 strategy — *"Offering transportation
  services or mobile donation units to increase accessibility."*
- **Streamlined processes:** [Mohammed] Cluster 3 strategy — *"Ensuring a quick and smooth
  donation process."* [Malhotra] also cites an Indian study recommending *"prior registration,
  online portal for donation registration and allocation of time slots will minimize donor
  waiting times."*

---

### 4.5 Handle Temporary Deferral Carefully

**Basis:** [Bagot]: *"Deferrals appear to be the strongest deterrent of repeat donations for
first-time donors."*

[van Dongen]: *"Temporary deferral for medical reasons, such as a low haemoglobin level,
can also cause donors to lapse. This effect is especially strong in first-time donors."*

**Recommended intervention:** Implementation intentions have been tested specifically for
deferred donors with a 19% improvement in return rates [van Dongen, citing Godin et al.
2013]. The paper also notes: *"how we can frame deferral messages to decrease lapsing after
temporary deferral, has not been studied yet."*

**Recommendation:** Indian blood banks should have a specific post-deferral outreach
protocol, incorporating structured planning prompts and positive, non-stigmatising messaging.

## Section 5: Messaging and Motivation

---

### 5.1 Use "Warm Glow" Messaging Rather Than Pure Altruism Appeals

**Basis:** [van Dongen]: *"Other studies found that donating blood was associated with
feelings of 'warm glow' (donating blood because it makes one feel good about themselves),
and found no evidence that they were motivated by empathic concerns. Therefore, retention
campaigns should focus on warm glow rather than on purely altruistic messages."*

**Indian data corroboration:** [Mohammed]: *"The primary motivation for blood donation among
participants is positive emotions (47.1 %), followed by personal reasons (28.0 %) and
prosocial reasons (23.5 %)."* The 47.1% "positive emotions" category aligns with what the
broader literature calls warm glow.

**Caveat:** [Malhotra] notes: *"All the participants irrespective of VD or RD donors
considered blood donation to be altruistic in the interview."* However, self-reported
altruism and actual predictors of behaviour are different — [van Dongen] cites evidence that
*"beliefs in personal rather than societal benefit predict actual future donation."*

**Recommendation:** Indian blood bank communications — post-donation messages, social
media, and reminders — should emphasise how the donor feels (pride, positive emotion,
personal benefit) rather than relying only on duty or altruism messaging.

---

### 5.2 Build Donor Role Identity Over Time

**Basis:** [van Dongen]: *"after approximately four donations, 'being a blood donor' becomes
a 'role identity'. Donor role identity refers to the match between being a blood donor and
the person's self-concept, and is a predictor of future blood donation."*

Donor stages identified by Masser et al. (2008), cited in [van Dongen]: *(1) initiation,
(2) maintenance, (3) habit formation, (4) habit, and (5) establishment of blood donor
identity formation.* Each stage requires a different retention strategy.

**Recommendation:** Early retention communications (donations 1–4) should explicitly
reinforce the donor's identity as a blood donor — for example, using language like "as a
blood donor, you..." rather than treating each donation as a standalone transaction.

---

### 5.3 Leverage Anticipated Regret

**Basis:** [van Dongen]: *"Anticipated negative emotions, also labelled as anticipated regret
(as measured by 'If during the next 6 months I did NOT give blood again …': '…I would regret
it', '…It would bother me', '…I would be disappointed') have also been shown to predict
donation behaviour, especially in experienced donors."*

**Recommendation:** Retention messaging for regular donors can include gentle reminders
of the value they provide and the regret they may feel if they do not return — this is
distinct from pressure or guilt, and must be carefully worded to avoid the counter-pressure
effect (see Section 3.1).

## Section 6: Incentives

---

### 6.1 Free Medical Tests — Strongest Non-Monetary Incentive in India

**Basis:** [Malhotra] (Indian data, n = 1,000): *"Nearly all (98.8%–99.5%) cited free of cost
medical tests to be the most effective incentive for motivating for blood donation."*

Tests valued included: *"hemogram, lipid profile, renal function tests, liver function tests,
blood glucose levels, thyroid profile, Prostate-Specific Antigen test (PSA) (in males), and
mammography (in females)."*

This finding was consistent across: voluntary vs. replacement donors, first-time vs. repeat
donors, all age groups, and all education levels. No statistically significant differences
between subgroups were found.

**Recommendation:** Offering a health checkup panel to all donors is the single most
broadly supported non-monetary incentive in the Indian literature reviewed.

---

### 6.2 Small Gifts — High Acceptance

**Basis:** [Malhotra]: *"distribution of small gifts (93%–99.5%)"* was ranked second overall.

Gender-specific preferences:
- Males: *"most important was T-shirts, followed by diaries, calendars, pen sets, cup sets,
  and glasses."*
- Females: *"preferred cup sets, diaries followed by pen sets and T-shirts."*

[Mohammed] also lists *"Non monetary Incentives"* as a motivating factor, though endorsed by
only 1.5% as a primary motivation — suggesting gifts support but do not drive donation.

**Recommendation:** Small branded gifts (T-shirts, diaries, pen sets) are well-received
across Indian donor groups and are appropriate as token recognition. Gifts alone will not
drive retention but complement other strategies.

---

### 6.3 Education Credits and School Admission Credits — Moderately Valued

**Basis:** [Malhotra]: *"education credits (65%–84%)"* and *"credits in school admission
(65%–75%)"* were ranked third and fourth.

*"86% of the FT donors and 76% of the RT donors opined that education credits to students
can play an important role in blood donor recruitment."*

**Implementation note:** [Malhotra] explicitly states these require external support:
*"Support from the central government, national health services, and local agencies is very
essential for the implementation of interventions as education credits, school admission
credits."* These cannot be implemented unilaterally by blood banks.

---

### 6.5 Incentives to Avoid or Use Cautiously

**Cash incentives — to be avoided:**
[Malhotra]: *"Various studies state that introduction of cash incentives not only encourage
high risk donors but also affect the sustainability of blood services."* And: *"as monetary
incentives entails the risk of commercializing the blood donation process and endanger blood
safety."*

**Incentives with poor performance in India:**
[Malhotra]: *"Incentives with little role were tax benefit, 24 h work off and movie tickets,
these may not contribute in achieving expected goals."*

**Counter-effect risk on regular donors:**
[Malhotra] citing another study: *"Some studies suggest that interventions may have a counter
effect on already motivated regular donors, so they should be rather aimed at irregular and
newly recruited donors."*

**Sustainability caution:**
[Malhotra]: *"Incentives should be introduced with caution as if the requirements are not met
anytime; it may have a demoralizing effect on donor's psychology which can prevent them from
donating blood. In addition, they may discourage the donors who are primarily driven by
altruism."*

---

### 6.6 Recognition Programmes

**Basis:** [Malhotra]: *"Small tokens of gratitude such as medals, badges instill pride in
blood donors and help in encouraging new donors, thereby facilitating VD donor pool."*
However, acceptance was mixed: only *"27%–48% of the donors advocated badges and
mementos."*

The paper recommends: *"subtle gestures as birthday wishes and honoring them on occasions
such as world blood donor day and national blood donation day greatly acknowledge their
efforts."*

A cited study found: *"most donors preferred a private rather than a public recognition."*
[Malhotra, citing Chmielewski 2012]

**Recommendation:** Personalised, private recognition (birthday messages, milestone
acknowledgements) is preferable to public ceremonies for most donors. Formal public
recognition programmes have lower and more variable acceptance.

## Section 7: Operational and Structural Strategies

---

### 7.1 Prefer In-House Donation Over Camps

**Basis:** [Malhotra]: *"Ninety-five percent preferred in house donation over camp. It may be
due to reason that they find it more organized, clean, and less crowded."*

[Mohammed] also found donation venue inconvenience (19.4%) and long waiting times (19.9%)
as significant deterrents.

**Recommendation:** Blood banks should prioritise improving the in-house donation
experience — cleanliness, organisation, reduced wait times — over expanding camp-based
collection alone.

---

### 7.2 Reduce Waiting Times Through Technology

**Basis:** [Malhotra] citing an Indian study: *"prior registration, online portal for donation
registration and allocation of time slots will minimize donor waiting times and may add to
their comfort, thereby encouraging VD donations."*

[Mohammed] Cluster 3 strategy: *"Ensuring a quick and smooth donation process."*

**Recommendation:** Online pre-registration and time slot allocation systems should be
implemented at Indian blood banks. This directly addresses the 19.9% of donors citing long
waiting times as a deterrent [Mohammed].

---

### 7.3 Provide Entertainment During Donation

**Basis:** [Malhotra]: *"45% considered entertainment facility (TV, music) necessary during
blood donation."* Acceptance was higher among graduates (57%) than primary-educated donors
(40%).

[van Dongen] notes passive distraction is a potential coping strategy: *"Coping strategies
could also incorporate passive distraction, for example getting the donor to read a book or
magazine, or making tablets available."*

**Caution:** [Bagot] found that *"piped music increased anxiety for first-time donors but not
for repeat donors."* Entertainment should therefore be donor-controlled or opt-in, especially
for first-time donors.

---

### 7.4 Build Peer Networks and Donor Clubs

**Basis:** [Mohammed], Cluster 6 strategy: *"Form blood donor clubs within institutions to
maintain a sense of community and shared purpose."* And: *"Recruitment of enthusiastic
student ambassadors or peer leaders to advocate for blood donation within their networks."*

[Grazzini]: *"Blood and plasma donation should be seen as an inclusive process, encompassing
not only individual-level factors but also the expression of socially determined commitments,
including social reciprocity."*

Students represent 30.4% of the [Mohammed] study population — making educational
institutions a primary venue for community-based retention.

**Recommendation:** Partner with NSS chapters, college unions, and student organisations
to establish formal blood donor clubs and peer ambassador programmes.

---

### 7.5 Leverage Corporate Social Responsibility for Business Donors

**Basis:** [Mohammed] Cluster 9 strategy: *"Highlighting blood donation as a meaningful and
impactful Corporate Social Responsibility (CSR) activity."*

Business donors make up 13.4% of the [Mohammed] study population.

**Recommendation:** Indian blood banks should develop a formal CSR engagement programme
targeting companies, framing workplace blood drives as a CSR initiative and providing
companies with impact reports post-drive.

---

## Section 8: Global Context for India

---

### 8.1 India's Position in the Global Blood Supply Landscape

**Basis:** [Grazzini], citing WHO Global Database on Blood Safety 2018 (171 reporting
countries):

- *"As of 2018, about 118.4 million blood donations are collected worldwide. 40% of these
  are collected in high-income countries (HIC), home to 16 % of the world's population."*
- Median whole blood donation rate per 1,000 population:
  - High-income countries: 31.5
  - Upper-middle-income countries: 15.9
  - Low-middle-income countries: 6.8
  - Low-income countries: 5

- *"In 56 countries, more than 50% of the blood supply is still dependent on family/replacement
  and paid blood donors (47 LMIC)."*

**Relevance to India:** [Malhotra]'s sample shows 70.4% replacement donors vs. 29.6%
voluntary donors — indicating India still has significant dependence on replacement donation,
consistent with the LMIC pattern described by [Grazzini].

---

### 8.2 Goal: Shift to Voluntary Non-Remunerated Donation

**Basis:** [Grazzini]: *"The ultimate goal is to create a solid and positive culture of voluntary
non-remunerated blood donation as a normal part of a healthy lifestyle."*

World Health Assembly resolution WHA63.12, cited in [Grazzini]: *"urges Member States to
pursue blood and plasma self-sufficiency through efficiently managed and sustainable blood
and plasma programs."*

**Recommendation:** All retention strategies should explicitly support the long-term shift
from replacement to voluntary donation in India. Incentive programmes should be designed
to motivate voluntary donors without commercialising the process.

## Section 9: Overarching Cautions

---

### 9.1 Self-Reported Motivators Are Not Reliable Predictors of Behaviour

**Basis:** [Bagot]: *"The current review revealed that self-reported motivators are typically
not effective, and most successful predictive factors identified have not been translated
into interventions."*

[van Dongen] similarly: *"many researchers have questioned whether altruism is an actual
motivator for behaviour, or a rationalisation of more selfish motives."*

**Implication:** Donor surveys that ask "why do you donate?" will likely report altruism and
prosocial reasons — but these do not predict who will return. Strategies should focus on
**observable, modifiable predictors** (adverse events, planning, phlebotomist interaction,
reminders) rather than being built solely on self-reported motivations.

---

### 9.2 Interventions Have Largely Been Tested Outside India

[van Dongen]: *"A lot of these interventions have only been tested once, and often in only one
country or in one blood collection establishment."*

[Bagot]: *"The first-time WB donor literature mainly comprises single studies, which preclude
definitive findings."*

Both Indian studies reviewed are single-centre studies with male-dominated samples.
[Malhotra] explicitly states: *"Long-term studies are required from our country to ascertain
the type of incentives which appeal to different segments of donors having different
psychological and behavior patterns with different educational, socioeconomic and cultural
backgrounds."*

**Implication:** Every strategy in this document should be piloted and evaluated locally
before full rollout. The evidence base specific to India remains limited.

---

### 9.3 Do Not Apply Too Much Pressure

**Basis:** [van Dongen]: *"too much perceived 'pressure to donate'... can have counteractive
effects on retention."*

A suggested framing to reduce perceived pressure: *"'We understand that it is not always easy
to make time for donating blood. That's why we really appreciate it if you make the effort to
donate.'"* [van Dongen, citing Boeschen Hospers et al. 2013]
