Predicting Blood Donors Using Machine Learning Techniques
2021 USA Machine Learning
Full Citation
Kauten, C., Qin, X., Liu, X., & Fanning, A. (2021). Predicting Blood Donors Using Machine Learning Techniques. Information Systems Frontiers. DOI: 10.1007/s10796-021-10149-1
Research Objective
Apply contemporary ML algorithms to model blood donor retention using large regional blood center data to design cost-effective outreach programs.
Country & Sample Size
USA — large regional blood center (exact n not disclosed in search results; described as "large US blood center")
Key Findings
Random Forest classifier achieves Matthews Correlation Coefficient (MCC) of 0.851 for donor retention prediction. Model enables targeted outreach. Donor transaction history is the primary data source.
Variables Used
  • Donation recency
  • Donation frequency
  • Total volume donated
  • Time since first donation
  • Demographic features (age, weight, sex, blood group)
Retention Strategy
Predictive scoring of each donor's probability to return; targeted outreach to high-risk lapse donors.
Dataset Feature Engineering
  • Months since last donation (recency)
  • Total donation count (frequency)
  • Cumulative volume (cc) donated
  • Tenure in months (first→now)
  • Binary return label (0/1)
ML / Statistical Methods
Random Forest (primary), comparison with other classifiers; Matthews Correlation Coefficient as evaluation metric (preferred over accuracy for imbalanced classes).
✓ Implementable from transaction history → View Paper (Springer)

Knowledge Discovery on RFM Model Using Bernoulli Sequence (RFMTC Model — UCI Blood Transfusion Dataset)
2008 Taiwan Foundational / ML
Full Citation
Yeh, I.-C., Yang, K.-J., & Ting, T.-M. (2009). Knowledge discovery on RFM model using Bernoulli sequence. Expert Systems with Applications, 36(3), 5866–5871. DOI: 10.1016/j.eswa.2008.07.018. Dataset: UCI ML Repository ID 176 — Blood Transfusion Service Center.
Research Objective
Derive the RFMTC model (augmented RFM with Time since first purchase and Churn probability) using Bernoulli sequences to predict whether a donor will donate at the next time window.
Country & Sample Size
Taiwan — n = 748 donors randomly selected from Blood Transfusion Service Center, Hsin-Chu City
Key Findings
RFMTC outperforms raw RFM in predicting next-donation probability. 76% of donors did not donate in the target month (class imbalance). Recency and frequency are the strongest predictors.
Variables Used
  • R: Months since last donation (range: 0.03–74.4, mean: 9.74)
  • F: Total number of donations (range: 1–50, mean: 5.51)
  • M: Total blood donated in cc (range: 250–12,500, mean: 1,378.68)
  • T: Months since first donation (range: 2.27–98.3, mean: 34.42)
  • C: Binary donation in March 2007 (0/1)
Retention Strategy
Score each donor using RFMTC; prioritize outreach to donors with high churn probability. Adjust formula dynamically to the blood bank's database.
Dataset Feature Engineering
  • recency_months
  • frequency_count
  • monetary_cc
  • tenure_months
  • churn_probability (derived)
  • avg_inter_donation_interval = tenure / frequency
ML / Statistical Methods
Bernoulli sequence probability model; Logistic Regression; CART Decision Tree; Neural Networks (benchmarked by subsequent researchers). Dataset publicly available at UCI ML Repository.
✓ Directly implementable from transaction logs → UCI Dataset + Paper

Effective Methods for Reactivating Inactive Blood Donors: A Stratified Randomised Controlled Study
2020 China RCT
Full Citation
Yang, J.O., Bei, C.H., Liang, H.Q., et al. (2020). Effective methods for reactivating inactive blood donors: a stratified randomised controlled study. BMC Public Health, 20, 475. DOI: 10.1186/s12889-020-08537-2
Research Objective
Assess efficacy and cost-effectiveness of telephone calls vs SMS reminders vs no intervention for reactivating inactive blood donors.
Country & Sample Size
China (Guangzhou) — n = 11,880 inactive donors (last donation Jan–Jun 2014); 3 groups; followed 1 year
Key Findings
Phone calls significantly increased re-donation (ATT analysis) vs SMS and control. SMS prompted donors to return sooner (within 6 months). Re-donation positively associated with: male gender, older age, previous donation history. SMS was more cost-effective than phone calls (ICER calculation). Time constraints = #1 self-reported deferral reason.
Variables Used
  • Last donation date (inactivity duration)
  • Gender
  • Age
  • Number of prior donations
  • Contact method (phone/SMS/none)
  • Re-donation within 6 or 12 months (outcome)
Retention Strategy
Altruistic appeal SMS for lapsed donors; phone calls for donors with >12 months inactivity. Stratify by gender and donation history.
Dataset Feature Engineering
  • months_since_last_donation
  • is_contacted_by_sms (binary)
  • is_contacted_by_phone (binary)
  • gender (binary: male=1)
  • prior_donation_count
  • redonated_6m (outcome label)
  • redonated_12m (outcome label)
ML / Statistical Methods
Intention-to-Treat (ITT) analysis; Average Treatment Effect on the Treated (ATT); Incremental Cost-Effectiveness Ratio (ICER); Logistic regression for predictor identification.
✓ Variables directly from donor records + outreach logs → View Paper (PMC)

Predicting Willingness to Donate Blood Based on Machine Learning: Two Recruitments During COVID-19 Outbreaks
2022 China ML · XGBoost · SVM
Full Citation
Liu, C., et al. (2022). Predicting willingness to donate blood based on machine learning: two blood donor recruitments during COVID-19 outbreaks. PLOS ONE / PMC9647248. DOI available via PubMed PMC9647248.
Research Objective
Develop and validate ML models to predict and rank experienced donors' willingness to donate using SMS recruitment data; compare against conventional recruitment method.
Country & Sample Size
China — n = 95,476 SMS recruitments and their donation outcomes (experienced donors only)
Key Findings
Top 3 predictors: (1) blood donation interval, (2) age, (3) donation frequency. XGBoost and SVM achieved best performance. XGBoost AUC = 0.809, accuracy = 0.815, F1 = 0.843. XGBoost hit-rate 1.25× higher than conventional method; combined XGBoost+SVM model hit-rate 1.80× higher. High-willingness group hit rate 1.96× over low-willingness group.
Variables Used (13 features)
  • Blood donation interval (recency)
  • Age
  • Donation frequency
  • Blood type
  • Prior response to SMS recruitment
  • Season/time of recruitment
  • Gender
  • COVID-19 period flag
Retention Strategy
Precision recruitment: score each eligible donor by predicted donation probability; target high-willingness donors first. Reduces wasted SMS outreach costs.
Dataset Feature Engineering
  • donation_interval_days (primary)
  • age_years
  • lifetime_donation_count
  • blood_type_encoded
  • sms_response_rate (prior campaigns)
  • season_of_recruitment (1–4)
  • willingness_score (0–1 float, target)
ML / Statistical Methods
XGBoost (best overall), SVM (best recall), Logistic Regression, Random Forest, Decision Tree, Naive Bayes, KNN — 7 models compared. AUC, accuracy, precision, recall, F1. PyCharm-Python environment.
✓ Directly from transaction + outreach history → View Paper (PMC)

Why Do Blood Donors Lapse or Reduce Their Donation's Frequency?
2016 Canada
Full Citation
Charbonneau, J., Cloutier, M.-S., & Carrier, É. (2016). Why do blood donors lapse or reduce their donation's frequency? Transfusion Medicine Reviews, 30(1), 1–5. DOI: 10.1016/j.tmrv.2015.12.001
Research Objective
Compare demographics and deterrents among plasma/platelet donors, regular whole blood donors, and lapsed whole blood donors in a voluntary non-remunerated system.
Country & Sample Size
Canada — Survey of 1,879 participants (207 regular WBDs, 148 PPDs, and lapsed donors). Data entry July 2014.
Key Findings
26% of WBDs and 31% of PPDs reduced donation frequency in past 5 years. Top lapse reasons: health reasons (39%), lack of information on blood drive locations/schedules (8%), fear of needles (4%). Sex, age group, and education significantly differ across donor segments. Separate retention plans recommended for male and female donors.
Variables Used
  • Donor type (WBD / PPD / lapsed)
  • Sex
  • Age group
  • Education level
  • Specific deterrent reasons (12 categories)
  • Frequency reduction over 5 years
Retention Strategy
Differentiated retention plans by gender; better location/schedule communication; address health-related deferral management.
Dataset Feature Engineering
  • lapsed_flag (binary)
  • gender
  • age_group
  • education_level
  • deterrent_category (encoded)
  • donation_type (whole blood / apheresis)
ML / Statistical Methods
Chi-square tests (2×3); descriptive statistics; SAS 9.4. Logistic regression applicable for lapse prediction using reported variables.
~ Partially implementable (requires survey data) → View Paper (ScienceDirect)

Efficacy of Communication Interventions for Promoting Blood Donation in LMICs: A Systematic Review
2025 LMIC (Multi-country)
Full Citation
[Authors] (2025). Efficacy of communication interventions for promoting blood donation in low- and middle-income countries: A systematic review. Vox Sanguinis. Published May 2025. DOI: 10.1111/vox... (PMC12116039)
Research Objective
Identify specific communication interventions and their efficacy for increasing blood donation in LMICs — directly applicable to India context.
Country & Sample Size
Systematic review (PRISMA); databases: PubMed, Scopus, PsycINFO, Web of Science, CINAHL, AJOL; includes studies from Africa, South Asia, Latin America
Key Findings
In university group (n=1,403 voluntary donors): 582 returned after receiving text messages while 870 donated without texts — text messages worked as a nudge but absolute numbers show walk-in > SMS in volume. Loss-framed SMS messages more effective than gain-framed (p=0.034) for Rh-D negative donors. Phone call + educational letters + incentives + group meetings all improved first-time → second donation conversion.
Variables Used

  • Message framing (loss vs gain)
  • Blood type (Rh-negative focus)
  • Donation status (first-time vs repeat)
  • Return rate within defined window
Retention Strategy
Loss-framed messaging for Rh-negative donors; multi-modal outreach (SMS + phone + education) for first-time donor conversion to repeat.
Dataset Feature Engineering
  • message_type_encoded
  • message_framing (loss=1/gain=0)
  • blood_group_rh_negative (binary)
  • outreach_channel_count
  • converted_to_repeat (outcome)
ML / Statistical Methods
Systematic review; meta-analysis where applicable; Chi-square tests in individual studies; logistic regression for predictor studies.

Adverse Events and Predisposing Factors in Voluntary and Replacement Whole Blood Donors — North India
2012 India (North)
Full Citation
Marwaha, N., Sharma, R.R. (2012). Analysis of adverse events and predisposing factors in voluntary and replacement whole blood donors: A study from north India. Asian Journal of Transfusion Science. PMC3439755. DOI: 10.4103/0973-6247.98930
Research Objective
Assess frequency of adverse events (AEs) in whole blood donors and analyse predisposing factors; establish their link to donor non-return (retention risk).
Country & Sample Size
India (Chandigarh, North India) — n = 37,896 donors over 14 months (Jan 2002–Feb 2003). VD = 22,587 (59.6%); RD = 15,309 (40.4%)
Key Findings
Overall reaction rate: 2.5%. VVR = 63.5% of all reactions; hematomas = 35.0%. VVR significantly associated with: young age, lower weight, first-time donor status, female gender, donation camp setting. 85% of VVR donors reported prior fear/anxiety. Male replacement donors had more reactions than voluntary donors (p=0.03). Hematoma formation more common with less-trained staff.
Variables Used
  • Donor type (voluntary vs replacement)
  • Donation status (first-time vs repeat)
  • Age
  • Weight
  • Gender
  • Donation setting (camp vs in-centre)
  • Adverse event type and grade
  • Pre-donation anxiety (self-reported)
Retention Strategy
Pre-donation counselling; identify at-risk donors (young, female, first-time, low weight); targeted support at camps; trained phlebotomy staff.
Dataset Feature Engineering
  • is_first_time_donor (binary)
  • age_years
  • weight_kg
  • gender
  • donation_setting (camp/centre)
  • adverse_event_flag (binary)
  • adverse_event_grade (ordinal 0–3)
ML / Statistical Methods
Chi-square tests; multivariate logistic regression for predisposing factors; frequency analysis. AE flag can serve as a retention risk feature in ML models.

Motivational Factors for Blood Donation, Potential Barriers, and Knowledge — First-Time and Repeat Blood Donors (Ghana)
2018 Ghana (LMIC)
Full Citation
Mohammed, S., & Essel, H.B. (2018). Motivational factors for blood donation, potential barriers, and knowledge about blood donation in first-time and repeat blood donors. BMC Hematology, 18, 36. DOI: 10.1186/s12878-018-0130-x
Research Objective
Compare motivators, barriers, and knowledge between first-time and repeat donors in a developing-country hospital blood bank setting analogous to India.
Country & Sample Size
Ghana (Tamale Teaching Hospital) — n = 350 donors; 192 (54.9%) first-time, 158 (45.1%) repeat; Jan–Feb 2018
Key Findings
90.3% motivated when someone they know needs blood (emergency appeal strongest motivator). Good staff attitude: 83.4% motivator; poor staff attitude: 63.7% deterrent. Privacy during screening: 54.6% deterrent. Concern blood may be sold: 50.9% deterrent. Only 36% knew minimum donation age; 37.7% knew annual donation limits — knowledge gap is a barrier to retention.
Variables Used
  • Donor status (first-time vs repeat)
  • 27 motivational/barrier items
  • Knowledge score (10+ items)
  • Sociodemographic factors
Retention Strategy
Staff attitude training; privacy assurance during screening; knowledge campaigns; emergency appeal messaging.
Dataset Feature Engineering
  • is_repeat_donor (binary)
  • motivation_score (composite)
  • knowledge_score
  • privacy_concern (binary)
  • emergency_appeal_responsive (binary)
ML / Statistical Methods
Chi-square tests for association between donor status and motivators/barriers/demographics; descriptive statistics; purposive sampling.
~ Partial — survey-based features → View Paper (PMC)

  • year

Developing a Commitment to Blood Donation: The Impact of One's First Experience (Identity Theory — Foundational)
1983 (Foundational) USA
Full Citation
Callero, P.L., & Piliavin, J.A. (1983). Developing a commitment to blood donation: The impact of one's first experience. Journal of Applied Social Psychology, 13(1), 1–16. DOI: 10.1111/j.1559-1816.1983.tb00883.x
Research Objective
Examine how the first donation experience shapes donor identity and long-term commitment, testing multiple psychological theories of donation behavior.
Country & Sample Size
USA — Longitudinal cohort of blood donors; multiple time-point tracking (American Red Cross sample; exact n varies by study wave)
Key Findings
External social pressures dominate early donation; self-originating factors dominate later. At least 3 donations needed before donors identify as "a blood donor" — identity integration threshold. Role identity as donor correlates with donation frequency. Cited in 1000+ subsequent studies; underpins all modern retention theory.
Variables Used
  • Number of prior donations
  • Donor identity score (role identity scale)
  • Motivation type (external vs internal)
  • Intention to donate again
  • Actual subsequent donation
Retention Strategy
First-donation experience management; reinforcing identity formation through recognition and communication; target donors approaching the 3-donation identity threshold.
Dataset Feature Engineering
  • donation_count_band (1 / 2 / 3 / 4+ bands — identity threshold)
  • is_committed_donor (≥3 donations binary)
  • first_donation_experience_score
  • internal_motivation_proxy (derived from recency + regularity)
ML / Statistical Methods
Longitudinal regression; path analysis (structural model); behavioral decision theory. The 3-donation threshold is a directly engineerable feature: `is_committed = (total_donations >= 3)`.
Indirect — identity threshold engineerable as feature → View Paper (Wiley)
